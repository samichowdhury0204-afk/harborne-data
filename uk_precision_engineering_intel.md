## 5. The 'good shop' archetype

A Tier 1 UK precision engineering site almost always has these elements, in roughly this order:

1. **A specific hero, not a generic one.** Not "Precision Engineering Solutions" — but "Aerospace Fine Limit Sheet Metalwork" or "Pendulum Roller Mill Experts" or "Component Manufacturer — High Precision: aerospace, defence, hydraulic and gas". A vertical or a process, named explicitly.
2. **A heritage line.** "Family-run since 1959" / "Third generation" / "Established 1974". This appears within the first viewport on ~80% of T1 sites. It is the dominant trust signal in this segment.
3. **Concrete proof numbers.** Sq ft, machine count, axes, components per year, max part dimensions, materials handled. T1 sites quote at least one. T2 sites quote zero.
4. **A capability matrix or services grid.** Usually 6–9 tiles: CNC milling, CNC turning, fabrication, welding, CMM inspection, finishing, assembly, etc. Often above the fold.
5. **Cert badges on the homepage**, not buried. ISO 9001 minimum; AS9100 / NADCAP / Cyber Essentials if they have them. Logos rather than text.
6. **Named industries served (the "Sectors" page).** A list of 4–8 verticals. Aerospace and defence top the list whenever truthfully claimable.
7. **Case studies or named clients** (rolled out as logo strips: BAE Systems, JLR, Rolls-Royce, Tarmac, Anglian Water, Mercedes AMG…).
8. **A contact form, not just a phone number** + visible UK postcode.
9. **An "About / Why us" page** with a photo of the workshop or the team.
10. **Tone:** confident but not slick. They sell on competence. They never use words like "synergy", "ecosystem", "enable", "leverage".

The pages that recur: **Home, About, Capabilities (or Services), Sectors (or Industries), Quality (or Accreditations), Case Studies, Contact**. That's the seven-page template. If you build websites for these firms, that template is your starting point.

## 6. Pain signals you can call out in cold emails

These are the things that are visibly wrong on T2/T3 sites — every one is a credibility leak you can name in your opening line:

- **Stale copyright dates** in the footer (© 2013, © 2017). Calls into question whether they still trade.
- **Stale ISO version** (9001:**2005** still on display when current is 2015). Aerospace buyers will silently disqualify them.
- **Theme placeholder text never replaced** — "Smart. Flexible. Beautiful. Divi is the most powerful theme in our collection" sitting in the hero. Saw this on multiple sites.
- **"Welcome to [Company Name]"** as the hero. Communicates nothing.
- **Wix / Squarespace / Hostinger sites that don't render readable HTML** — meaning Google can't index them either, and neither can ChatGPT/Gemini when buyers search there.
- **No certifications visible above the fold**, even when the company actually holds ISO 9001.
- **No phone-number-AND-form combo** — just a phone number, or just an info@ email.
- **No specific capability numbers** — no max part size, no axis count, no materials list, no batch range. Just adjectives.
- **Stock photo of generic gears or sparks** instead of a real workshop photo.
- **News page where the most recent post is from 2015–2018.**
- **Cookie consent banner from 2010s** (pre-GDPR styling).
- **Expired SSL certs** (saw bauercoating.co.uk).
- **No HTTP→HTTPS redirect**, or the site only works on http://www.x not https://x — saw this 4–5 times. Modern browsers warn buyers.
- **No mobile menu / unscrollable on mobile.**
- **Generic stock photos with no people in them** + "Family-run" claim. Cognitive dissonance.
- **Industries listed but no proof of working in them** ("we serve aerospace" with no client logos or AS9100 cert).

## 7. Recommended offer phrasing — 3 alternatives

You're pitching **commission-only outbound** to founders of small UK precision engineering shops. They are trade-skilled, sceptical of marketing people, time-starved, and have been pitched (occasionally) by SEO agencies who didn't understand their work. The trick: sound like you understand the *trade*, not the marketing. All three options below front-load that.

**Option A — "I find you orders" (transactional, shop-floor language)**

> Subject: extra orders, no fee unless they pay
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> I work with UK precision engineering shops finding new contract work — aerospace, automotive, medical, hydraulics. I don't charge a retainer; I take a small cut of any order I bring you that you accept.
>
> If you've got open capacity on the CNC side or fabrication this quarter, I can quietly reach out to OEMs and tier-1s on your behalf and put quotes in front of them. You only pay when something lands.
>
> Worth a 10-minute call to see what kit you've got and what work fits?
>
> [Your name]

**Why this works:** "orders" is the word *they* use, not "leads". "Open capacity" is the constraint they actually feel. "Quietly reach out" reassures them you won't embarrass them. Commission-only is stated upfront so they don't feel like they're being sold to.

**Option B — "I noticed [specific thing]" (consultative, opens with proof you looked)**

> Subject: your aerospace page
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> Quick one — saw [Company] on Companies House looking for similar shops to a project I'm running. Had a poke around your site. The aerospace work is clearly there but it doesn't lead the homepage, and I think buyers searching for AS9100 fine-limit machining in [region] are bouncing past you.
>
> I help small UK precision shops bring in extra contract work on commission only — no retainer, no upfront cost, you pay me a small % of any order you actually accept. I do the prospecting; you do what you're good at.
>
> Worth a quick call?
>
> [Your name]

**Why this works:** Names a specific weakness from their *actual* site, which proves you looked, which earns you the next 10 seconds. The pivot to commission-only is then framed as "a way to fix that without spending money".

**Option C — "Empty CNC capacity" (the problem they have, named directly)**

> Subject: 5-axis capacity sitting idle?
>
> Hi [Name],
>
> If you've got machine hours that aren't booked this quarter, I'd like to fill them.
>
> I work with small UK precision shops finding contract work directly from buyers — aerospace, medical, motorsport, oil & gas. No retainer, no setup fee, commission-only on accepted orders. I do the calling, the quoting handover comes back to you.
>
> Reply 'capacity' if you're interested and I'll send through a 1-pager on how it works.
>
> [Your name]

**Why this works:** It opens with the *most* painful version of their problem (idle machines = lost margin = the thing that keeps them up). It's also the shortest — busy founders read short emails. The "reply 'capacity'" is a low-friction CTA.

**Which to send:** A/B them. My gut says Option C wins on reply rate to busy owner-operators (T2/T3 shops); Option B wins on quality of replies from more sophisticated T1 shops. A is the safe middle.

## 8. Your homepage copy

Your value prop, written in their language:

> # Extra orders for UK precision shops.
> ## Commission-only outbound. We bring you contract work; you only pay when an order lands.
>
> **Who we work with:** UK CNC machining, fabrication, sheet metal, treatment & coating, and toolmaking shops — the small and mid-size firms doing aerospace, defence, automotive, medical, and oil & gas work. Family-run, owner-operated, ISO 9001-certified shops with open capacity and no time to chase new buyers.

**Why this wording:**
- "Extra orders" beats "lead generation" by a mile in this segment. They don't need leads, they need orders.
- "Commission-only" goes in the subhead because it's *the* differentiator vs the SEO/PPC agencies that have already pitched them.
- "Only pay when an order lands" mirrors how they think about their own contracts.
- The "Who we work with" line uses their own SIC categories in plain English, then names the certs and verticals so readers self-identify in the first sentence.
- "Open capacity" is the dog-whistle phrase. Anyone in the trade reads "open capacity" and immediately knows you've worked in or around shops before.

**Optional sub-block — "How it works" (3 steps):**
> 1. **You tell us your kit and capacity.** What you can make, what materials you handle, what's free this quarter.
> 2. **We find buyers.** Direct outbound to OEMs and tier-1s — aerospace, automotive, medical, defence. We hand over the conversation when they're ready to RFQ.
> 3. **You pay only when an order is accepted.** A small commission on the first invoice. No retainer, no setup fee, no contract.

## 10. Three things to do this week

1. **Send Option C (the "idle capacity" email) to your 100 most relevant Tier 2/3 shops** — the ones whose websites show real work but read like the site is from 2014. They feel the pain most, they can't fix it themselves, and they have the lowest sales-pitch fatigue.
2. **Build your homepage in the language above.** No "platform", no "growth", no "AI". The phrase "extra orders" should appear in the first viewport.
3. **For your top 20 prospects, harvest one specific pain signal from their site** (stale ISO version, Divi placeholder, broken https, old news, hidden certs) and put it in your subject line. Reply rates on these will be 5–10× the generic version. The list above gives you the pattern; spend 60 seconds per prospect in their footer/about page.
